⚠ How the real QAS test actually works Questions are almost always presented as real-world word problems or scenario-based situations — not bare equations. You might be asked about a plumber's hourly rate, a graph of a car's fuel usage, a recipe ratio, or a probability situation. You need to read carefully, identify what is being asked, and set up the math yourself. This tool is built to match that format exactly.

🔎 What the QAS test covers

The test has 21 multiple-choice questions spread across six skill areas:

  • Rational numbers — fractions, decimals, percents, absolute value, number operations
  • Ratios & proportional reasoning — unit rates, proportions, percent change, unit conversions
  • Linear equations & expressions — writing and solving equations from word problems, inequalities
  • Graphing & linear functions — slope, y-intercept, reading graphs, interpreting linear models
  • Probability & statistics — basic probability, mean/median/mode, interpreting data displays
  • Geometry & measurement — area, perimeter, volume, the Pythagorean theorem, coordinate geometry

⚠ The most important thing to understand about this test

The QAS test rarely asks you to "solve 4x − 5 = 19." Instead it says something like: "A contractor charges $45 per hour plus a $65 travel fee. A customer's bill was $245. How many hours did the contractor work?" You have to read the scenario, identify what is unknown, set up the equation, and solve it. Reading carefully and translating words into math is the core skill.

📖 Key concept: translating words into math

Every topic on the QAS shows up in word-problem form. Before you can solve, you have to translate. Study the phrase-to-symbol conversions in the Strategy tab alongside these concept cards.

Rational numbers & operations
TestedFractions, decimals, percents & absolute value⌄
Converting: Fraction → decimal: divide top by bottom (3/4 = 0.75). Decimal → percent: multiply by 100 (0.75 = 75%). Percent → decimal: divide by 100 (35% = 0.35).

Absolute value |x| = the distance from zero — always positive or zero. |−7| = 7. |7| = 7.

Operations with negatives: negative × negative = positive. negative × positive = negative.
Real test style: The temperature dropped from −3°F to −18°F. What is the absolute difference?
|−18 − (−3)| = |−18 + 3| = |−15| = 15°F ✓
Ratios, rates & proportions
Very commonSetting up and solving proportions⌄
A proportion says two ratios are equal. Cross-multiply to solve. Always label what each number represents so you set it up correctly.
a/b = c/d → a × d = b × c
Real test style: A recipe uses 3 cups of oats for every 2 cups of honey. How many cups of honey are needed for 10 cups of oats?
3/2 = 10/x → 3x = 20 → x = 20/3 ≈ 6.67 cups ✓
Very commonPercent change & unit conversions⌄
% change = (New − Old) ÷ Old × 100
Unit rate = total amount ÷ number of units
Real test style: A store raised a price from $40 to $52. What was the percent increase?
(52 − 40) ÷ 40 × 100 = 12 ÷ 40 × 100 = 30% increase ✓
Linear equations & expressions
Most testedWriting equations from word problems⌄
Step 1: Identify the unknown — write "let x = ___"
Step 2: Translate key phrases into math operations
Step 3: Write the equation
Step 4: Solve, then verify your answer makes sense in context
Real test style: A cell phone plan charges $25/month plus $0.10 per text. Last month the bill was $43. How many texts were sent?
Let t = number of texts
25 + 0.10t = 43 → 0.10t = 18 → t = 180 texts ✓
CommonInequalities in context⌄
Solve like equations — except flip the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative.

"At least" → ≥   "At most" / "no more than" → ≤   "More than" → >   "Less than" → <
Real test style: A student needs to earn at least 360 points over 4 tests to get a B. She scored 82, 91, and 88. What is the minimum score she needs on the 4th test?
82 + 91 + 88 + x ≥ 360 → 261 + x ≥ 360 → x ≥ 99 ✓
Graphs & linear functions
Very commonSlope & y-intercept in real-world context⌄
y = mx + b   (m = slope/rate, b = starting value/y-intercept)
Slope = (y₂ − y₁) ÷ (x₂ − x₁) = rise ÷ run
On the QAS, slope and y-intercept are almost always described as real-world rates. Slope = the rate of change (cost per hour, miles per gallon). Y-intercept = the starting value (flat fee, initial amount).
Real test style: A graph shows a taxi fare. The line crosses the y-axis at $2.50 and passes through (4, $10.50). What is the cost per mile?
Slope = (10.50 − 2.50) ÷ (4 − 0) = 8 ÷ 4 = $2.00 per mile ✓
Probability & statistics
TestedProbability & mean/median in context⌄
Probability = favorable outcomes ÷ total possible outcomes
Mean = sum of values ÷ count of values
Median = middle value when sorted (avg of two middle if even)
Real test style: A bag has 4 red, 6 blue, and 2 yellow chips. One chip is drawn at random. What is the probability it is NOT red?
Total = 12. P(red) = 4/12. P(NOT red) = 1 − 4/12 = 8/12 = 2/3 ✓
Geometry & measurement
TestedArea, perimeter, volume & Pythagorean theorem⌄
Rectangle: Area = l×w   Perimeter = 2l+2w
Triangle: Area = ½×b×h
Circle: Area = πr²   Circumference = 2πr
Pythagorean theorem (right triangles): a²+b²=c²
Real test style: A rectangular floor is 14 ft long and 9 ft wide. Tile costs $3.50 per sq ft. What is the total cost to tile the floor?
Area = 14 × 9 = 126 sq ft. Cost = 126 × $3.50 = $441 ✓

📝 Step-by-step approach for every QAS word problem

  1. Read the whole problem once before doing any math — understand the situation.
  2. Identify what the question is actually asking — underline the question sentence.
  3. Name the unknown — write "let x = ___" before writing any equation.
  4. Translate words into math — use the key phrase list below.
  5. Solve, then check — does your answer make sense in the real-world context?
  6. Eliminate wrong answer choices first — if two look close, re-read the question.
Key phrase translations
is / was / equals / will be / costs → =
sum / total / increased by / more than / added to → +
difference / less than / decreased by / reduced by → −
product / times / of / multiplied by / per → ×
quotient / divided by / ratio of / out of / split evenly → ÷
at least / minimum / no less than → ≥
at most / maximum / no more than / cannot exceed → ≤
more than / exceeds / greater than → >
fewer than / less than / under → <
Reading graphs and tables — quick checklist
✓ Read the title first — what is being measured?
✓ Read the axis labels — what do x and y represent? What are the units?
✓ Find the slope (rate of change) between two labeled points
✓ Find the y-intercept — what is the starting value when x = 0?
✓ Only use what the graph/table shows — don\'t add outside information
Night-before cheat sheet

Proportions

  • Set up: a/b = c/d
  • Cross-multiply: ad = bc
  • Label every number

% change

  • (New−Old)÷Old×100
  • Increase: New > Old
  • Decrease: New < Old

Slope

  • m = (y₂−y₁)÷(x₂−x₁)
  • Slope = rate of change
  • y-intercept = start value

Linear equations

  • y = mx + b
  • m = rate (per hour, per mile)
  • b = flat fee or start

Probability

  • P = favorable ÷ total
  • P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
  • Always 0 to 1

Geometry

  • Rect area = l×w
  • Circle area = πr²
  • Pyth: a²+b²=c²

Mean/Median

  • Mean = sum÷count
  • Sort first for median
  • Outlier pulls mean

Adaptive test tip

  • Take time on first 5–8
  • Slow = more accurate

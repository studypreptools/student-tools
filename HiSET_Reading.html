Reading Strategies

Start here every study session. These are ranked by your biggest gaps first.

🔴 Synthesis & Generalization Priority 1
What are Synthesis questions?

These questions give you two passages on the same topic and ask you to compare them, draw a conclusion that covers both, or make a prediction. You must pull ideas from multiple places — not just one paragraph.

Strategy: The "Both Passages" Test

When you see a synthesis question, ask: Does this answer choice apply to BOTH passages? Wrong answers usually only describe one passage. The correct answer works for both.

  • Read Passage 1 → write one sentence about its main point
  • Read Passage 2 → write one sentence about its main point
  • Ask: "What do both passages agree on? What's different?"
  • Choose the answer that honestly covers both
Strategy: Drawing Conclusions from Evidence

These questions say things like: "Based on both passages, what can be concluded?" The answer must be supported by the text — not just something that sounds reasonable.

  • Underline 2–3 specific details (one from each passage if possible)
  • Eliminate answers that go too far beyond what the text actually says
  • Eliminate answers that only use one passage
  • Pick the answer that your underlined evidence directly supports
Strategy: Making Predictions

"What would the author most likely do next?" or "What would happen if...?" Stay inside the text's logic. The prediction must follow naturally from the evidence — not be a guess.

  • Find the author's main argument or attitude
  • Ask: "What action is consistent with this attitude?"
  • Eliminate anything that contradicts the author's point of view
Key question words for Synthesis (watch for these on the test):
  • "Both passages suggest that…"
  • "Compared to Passage 1, Passage 2…"
  • "Which statement would both authors most likely agree with?"
  • "Based on both passages, what can be concluded?"
  • "How do the authors differ in their approach to…?"
🟡 Comprehension Priority 2
Strategy: Stay in the Text

Comprehension questions ask about things the text explicitly states. The answer is always there — you just have to find it. Never answer from memory or opinion.

  • When you read a question, go back to the passage before answering
  • Find the sentence or paragraph that contains the answer
  • Eliminate choices that sound good but aren't in the text
Strategy: Vocabulary from Context Clues

"What does the word _____ mean as it is used in the passage?" Don't rely on what you already think the word means — the passage tells you.

  • Cover the word and read the full sentence
  • Predict your own meaning from context
  • Substitute each answer choice back into the sentence
  • Pick the one that makes the most sense in context
Strategy: Word Choice and Tone

"Why does the author use the word _____ here?" Think about the feeling or attitude that word creates. Is it positive/negative? Urgent/calm? Formal/casual?

🔵 Analysis Priority 3
Strategy: Finding the Main Idea

The main idea is what the whole passage is about — not just one paragraph. It's the author's central point.

  • Read the first and last paragraphs carefully — main idea is often there
  • Ask: "What is the author trying to tell me overall?"
  • Eliminate answers that are too specific (one detail) or too broad (not in the text)
  • The correct answer covers the whole passage, not just one part
Strategy: Author's Purpose

Every author writes for a reason: to inform, to persuade, to entertain, or to describe. Look at the language and type of text.

  • Informational/news article → to inform
  • Editorial/opinion piece → to persuade
  • Story or memoir → to entertain or describe
  • Check: does the author use facts, opinions, or emotions?
Strategy: Text Structure

Recognizing how a text is organized helps you find answers faster.

  • Chronological: events in time order → look for dates, "first/then/finally"
  • Cause & Effect: why something happened → "because, as a result, therefore"
  • Compare & Contrast: similarities and differences → "however, on the other hand, similarly"
  • Problem & Solution: a problem is stated, then a solution is offered
🟢 Inference & Interpretation Maintain
Strategy: Reading Between the Lines

Inference questions ask what the author implies but doesn't directly say. The answer is supported by clues in the text — but you have to connect the dots.

  • Find 2 or more clues in the text that point to an answer
  • Eliminate answers that have no textual support
  • Eliminate answers that go too far beyond what is suggested
  • The correct answer is always the most reasonable interpretation of the evidence
Strategy: Figurative Language

Simile (like/as), metaphor (says one thing IS another), personification (gives human qualities to non-human things), symbolism (object represents an idea).

Ask: What feeling or idea does this create? The answer is always about meaning, not the literal words.

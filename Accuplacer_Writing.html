📝 What you will see on the real test

The Accuplacer Next-Gen Writing test has 25 questions, all based on written passages. Here is the exact format:

  1. You are shown a passage of 300–350 words — an essay, article, or informational piece written by someone else.
  2. Certain words, phrases, and sentences in the passage are numbered or underlined.
  3. Each question asks you to make an editing decision about a specific part of that passage.
  4. There are 5 questions per passage, and you see the passage the entire time you answer those 5 questions.
  5. The test is adaptive — harder questions appear when you answer correctly.

⚠ The biggest difference from isolated sentence practice

  • You must read the whole passage first to understand the topic, tone, and flow.
  • Many questions can only be answered correctly if you understand the surrounding sentences — not just the one being edited.
  • Transition questions depend on knowing what came before AND after the blank.
  • Sentence placement questions require you to understand the logical flow of the whole paragraph.
  • "No change" is a valid answer on every question — sometimes the original is already correct.

Standard English conventions
Very commonSubject-verb agreement⌄
The verb must match its subject. Trick: cross out the "of the…" phrase to find the real subject. Words that are always singular: everyone, each, someone, nobody, either, neither, anything.
✗ Wrong
The collection of paintings are displayed in the hall.
✓ Right
The collection of paintings is displayed in the hall.
Cross out "of paintings" → "The collection IS" ✓
Very commonComma splices & run-ons⌄
A comma splice uses only a comma to join two complete sentences. A run-on joins two sentences with no punctuation at all. Fix with: comma + FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so), a semicolon, or two separate sentences.
✗ Comma splice
The program was effective, it helped hundreds of students.
✓ Fixed
The program was effective, and it helped hundreds of students.
Very commonDangling & misplaced modifiers⌄
The describing phrase must sit right next to the word it describes. Ask: "Who is doing the action at the start of the sentence?" That person must immediately follow the comma.
✗ Dangling
Having finished the report, the printer was used to print it.
✓ Fixed
Having finished the report, she used the printer to print it.
Very commonTransition words — match the relationship⌄
Always read the sentence before AND after the blank. Decide the logical relationship, then choose the matching word.
Contrast: however, nevertheless, although, yet, on the other hand
Cause/effect: therefore, as a result, consequently, thus
Addition: furthermore, in addition, also, moreover
Example: for example, for instance, specifically
Sequence: first, then, next, finally, subsequently
Very common"No change" — don't fix what isn't broken⌄
Every question includes a "No change" option. Many students lose points by changing things that were already correct. Before picking any answer, ask: "Can I actually name the specific error here?" If you can't name it, the original is probably fine — choose "No change."
CommonParallel structure⌄
All items in a list or series must use the same grammatical form.
✗ Not parallel
The program focuses on teaching reading, to improve writing, and math skills.
✓ Parallel
The program focuses on teaching reading, improving writing, and building math skills.
CommonVerb tense consistency⌄
Don't switch tenses without a logical reason. Find the passage's main tense and stay in it. If the surrounding sentences are in past tense, the answer should be in past tense too.
✗ Inconsistent
The researcher gathered her data and analyzes the results.
✓ Consistent
The researcher gathered her data and analyzed the results.
CommonSentence placement & relevance⌄
Placement: Read the passage without the sentence. Find where it follows logically from the sentence before it AND leads naturally into the sentence after it.

Relevance: Ask "Does this sentence directly support the paragraph's main idea?" If not — delete it. Even interesting facts should be cut if they don't support the focus.
CommonPrecise word choice & conciseness⌄
Choose the most specific and clear option. Avoid vague words ("good," "things," "stuff") and wordy phrases ("due to the fact that" = "because"). The best answer says the most with the fewest words without losing meaning.
✗ Vague and wordy
The results had a good effect on the situation in a positive way.
✓ Precise and concise
The results significantly improved patient outcomes.
Night-before cheat sheet

Comma splices

  • Comma alone = NOT enough
  • Comma + FANBOYS = OK
  • Semicolon alone = OK
  • Period + new sentence = OK

Subject-verb

  • Cross out "of the…"
  • Find the REAL subject
  • Singular subject = singular verb
  • Everyone/each = singular

Modifiers

  • Who is doing the action?
  • That person follows the comma
  • A thing following = dangling

Transitions

  • Read BOTH surrounding sentences
  • Contrast → however
  • Result → therefore
  • Add → furthermore

Placement Qs

  • Read without the sentence first
  • Where does it fit logically?
  • Must connect before AND after

No change

  • Always a valid choice
  • Name the error first
  • If you can't name it → No change

⚠ The most important shift in mindset

On isolated sentence practice, you only look at one sentence. On the real test, every question is connected to a full passage. You must understand the whole piece of writing before you can edit any part of it correctly. Always read the entire passage before answering question 1.

Step-by-step approach for every passage
  1. Read the entire passage first — don't answer anything yet. Get the topic, tone, and overall flow.
  2. Identify the main idea of each paragraph as you read.
  3. Now go to Question 1. Read it carefully — what is it actually asking?
  4. Go back to the passage and read the surrounding sentences — not just the targeted one.
  5. Eliminate obviously wrong answers first, then compare your top two choices.
  6. Read your chosen answer back into the passage. Does the passage flow naturally with it?
  7. If you can't name a specific error in the original — choose "No change."
Question-type specific strategies
Transition questions
Read the sentence BEFORE the blank and the sentence AFTER. Decide: are these ideas adding, contrasting, or cause-effect? Then pick the matching word. Don't pick a word just because it sounds formal.
Sentence placement questions
Read the passage without the sentence. Try each location — where does the sentence follow naturally from what came before AND lead into what comes next? That's the right spot.
Delete or keep questions
Ask: "Does this sentence directly support the main point of this paragraph?" If it's off-topic, repetitive, or only loosely related — delete it. Even interesting facts get cut if they don't support the focus.
Grammar questions
Read the whole sentence — and the ones around it for tense context. Use the ear test: say the sentence in your head. If it sounds wrong, it probably is. Can you name the specific error? If not, consider "No change."

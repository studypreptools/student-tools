Social Studies Strategies

Most questions have a stimulus. Read it carefully before looking at the choices.

💡 Universal Strategies (all questions)
Read the stimulus first — the answer is usually there

Before reading the question, look at the source title, any labels, dates, and the overall format. Then read the stimulus. Then read the question. Finally, find the answer choice that matches what the stimulus actually says — not what you remember from history class.

Fact vs. Opinion vs. Conclusion
  • Fact: can be verified as objectively true ("The Civil War ended in 1865")
  • Opinion: a personal belief or value judgment ("The Civil War was unnecessary")
  • Conclusion: a logical inference drawn from evidence ("Because the North had more industry, it had a military advantage")
Identify author purpose and bias
  • Who created this source? When? Why?
  • Who is the intended audience?
  • What does the author want the reader to think or feel?
  • What information might be left out?
Evaluate conclusions carefully
  • Conclusions must be supported by the evidence — not go beyond it
  • Watch for words like "always," "never," "proves," "solely" — these are often too absolute
  • The best answer is the most directly and completely supported by the stimulus
📄 I. History — 35% of the test
Primary source documents
  • Identify: Who wrote it? When? What is their position or perspective?
  • Find the main argument or point in the last 1–2 sentences of each paragraph
  • Watch for emotionally loaded language — it signals the author’s bias
  • Every quote question: the answer is IN the quote. Don’t use outside knowledge.
Political cartoons
  • Identify every labeled symbol — each label is a clue to meaning
  • Ask: what is exaggerated? Exaggeration = the cartoonist’s criticism
  • Ask: what is the overall message these symbols send together?
  • Cartoon answers are usually critical of someone or something — find who/what
Key eras to know
  • American Revolution: taxation without representation, Enlightenment ideas, Constitution
  • Civil War & Reconstruction: slavery, 13th/14th/15th Amendments
  • WWI: MAIN causes (Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism); Treaty of Versailles
  • Great Depression: 1929 crash, New Deal programs (Social Security, CCC, FDIC)
  • WWII: Allies vs. Axis, Holocaust, atomic bomb, UN formed after
  • Cold War: U.S. vs. USSR, proxy wars, nuclear arms race, 1991 end
⚖ II. Civics & Government — 35% of the test
Three branches — know every function
  • Legislative (Congress): MAKES laws; has Senate (100) and House (435)
  • Executive (President): ENFORCES laws; can veto bills; commands military
  • Judicial (Supreme Court): INTERPRETS laws; can strike down unconstitutional laws
Checks and balances — key examples
  • President vetoes a law → Congress can override with 2/3 vote in both chambers
  • Supreme Court can strike down laws as unconstitutional (judicial review)
  • Senate must confirm presidential appointments (judges, Cabinet members)
  • Congress declares war; President commands the military
Bill of Rights — most tested amendments
  • 1st: speech, religion, press, peaceful assembly, petition
  • 4th: protection from unreasonable search and seizure
  • 5th: due process; cannot be forced to testify against yourself
  • 6th: right to a speedy and public trial, legal counsel
  • 14th: equal protection under the law; citizenship for all born in the U.S.
Federalism: federal vs. state powers
  • Federal only: declare war, print money, regulate interstate commerce, foreign policy
  • State only: education policy, driver’s licenses, local law enforcement, marriage laws
  • Shared: taxation, building roads, borrowing money
📈 III. Economics — 20% of the test
Supply and demand
  • Demand up + supply same = price up
  • Supply up + demand same = price down
  • Demand up + supply down = price rises sharply
  • Competition increases supply → prices fall
Fiscal vs. monetary policy
  • Fiscal policy = Congress + President: government spending and taxation
  • Expansionary fiscal: increase spending OR cut taxes → stimulate economy
  • Monetary policy = Federal Reserve: controls interest rates and money supply
  • Raise rates → borrow less → spend less → slows inflation
  • Lower rates → borrow more → spend more → stimulates economy
Consumer economics
  • Credit = get it now, pay more in total (interest adds to the cost)
  • Saving = wait longer, pay less in total
  • Tariff = tax on imports, protects domestic industry but raises consumer prices
  • Opportunity cost = what you give up to make a choice
🌎 IV. Geography — 10% of the test
Reading maps
  • Always check: title, legend/key, scale, compass rose (N/S/E/W)
  • The answer to a map question is almost always in the legend
  • Don’t assume — read what the map actually shows
Push vs. pull factors in migration
  • PUSH = negative conditions that drive people AWAY from a place (war, famine, poverty, persecution)
  • PULL = positive conditions that attract people TO a place (jobs, safety, family, better wages)
  • Most migrations involve both push and pull factors working together

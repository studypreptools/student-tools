Writing Strategies

Most questions ask you to REVISE a draft — not write from scratch. Read the whole passage first for context.

💡 Universal Strategies
Read the whole passage first

Before answering any questions about a passage, read the entire text. Understanding the overall meaning, tone, and structure helps you make better revision decisions for individual sentences.

Read the sentence in context

Always read the sentence BEFORE and AFTER the underlined portion. Many errors — especially transitions, pronoun reference, and tense — only become visible when you see the surrounding sentences.

Eliminate wrong answers first
  • Eliminate choices that introduce NEW errors
  • Eliminate choices that change the meaning of the sentence
  • Eliminate choices that are awkward or wordy when a simpler option exists
  • The correct answer fixes the problem WITHOUT creating a new one
📄 I. Organization of Ideas — 22%
Transitions — identify the relationship first
  • Contrast: however, although, on the other hand, yet, but, despite
  • Addition: furthermore, in addition, also, moreover, additionally
  • Result/Cause: therefore, consequently, as a result, thus
  • Time: meanwhile, subsequently, previously, at the same time
  • Example: for instance, for example, specifically, in particular
Relevance — does this sentence belong?
  • Ask: does this sentence directly support the main idea of the paragraph?
  • If it introduces a completely unrelated topic → remove it
  • If it contradicts the paragraph’s argument without purpose → remove it
  • Be careful: a sentence that seems off-topic might connect if you read ahead
Sentence placement
  • Look for transition words as clues: “As a result” must follow a cause
  • Look for pronoun references: “This idea” must follow the idea being referenced
  • Test each position by reading the sentences before and after
✏ II. Language Facility — 43%
Parallel structure — match the form
  • Find the FIRST item in the list and identify its form (gerund, infinitive, noun, clause)
  • Make ALL other items match that exact form
  • Common pairs: not only...but also, both...and, either...or, neither...nor
  • WRONG: She likes swimming, to run, and hikes. RIGHT: She likes swimming, running, and hiking.
Modifiers — who is doing the action?
  • The subject RIGHT AFTER the comma must be the one doing the introductory action
  • WRONG: Running down the street, the rain soaked us. (Rain wasn’t running)
  • RIGHT: Running down the street, we got soaked by the rain.
  • If no logical subject follows the phrase → dangling modifier. Rewrite the whole sentence.
Tone and word choice
  • Match the formality level to the context (business letter = formal, diary = informal)
  • Choose precise words over vague ones (declared > said; exhausted > tired)
  • Avoid wordiness — if two words do the same job, cut one
  • Consistency: don’t shift from formal to casual within the same piece
Comparative vs. superlative
  • Comparing TWO things: comparative (-er or more): faster, more effective
  • Comparing THREE or MORE: superlative (-est or most): fastest, most effective
  • For words with 3+ syllables: ALWAYS use more/most, never add -er/-est
✓ III. Writing Conventions — 35%
Subject-verb agreement traps
  • Singular subjects: each, every, either, neither, anyone, everyone, someone → singular verb
  • Don’t be fooled by prepositional phrases: “Each of the students IS...” (not “are”)
  • Compound subjects with “and” = plural. With “or/nor” = match the nearest subject
Run-ons and fragments
  • Run-on fix: (1) period, (2) semicolon, (3) comma + FANBOYS conjunction
  • Comma splice: two independent clauses with ONLY a comma = always wrong
  • Fragment: starts with because, although, when, if, since, while → needs an independent clause
  • Test: can it stand alone as a complete sentence? If no → fragment
Semicolon and conjunctive adverbs
  • SEMICOLON + however/therefore/moreover/furthermore + COMMA
  • This is one of the most tested punctuation patterns on the HiSET
  • WRONG: I studied hard, however I failed. RIGHT: I studied hard; however, I failed.
Pronoun rules
  • Subject pronouns (I, he, she, we, they): used as the subject of a verb
  • Object pronouns (me, him, her, us, them): used as objects of verbs or prepositions
  • WRONG: Him and me went. RIGHT: He and I went. WRONG: Give it to he. RIGHT: Give it to him.
  • Pronoun-antecedent: “each student” is singular → use his or her (not their)
✍ Essay Strategies
Before you write — spend 5 minutes planning
  • Read both passages and mark the key points in each
  • Decide your position BEFORE writing the intro
  • Jot down 2–3 reasons that support your position
  • Note which passage supports your argument and which one you need to address
Essay structure — five paragraphs
  • Paragraph 1: Introduction — state your position clearly (thesis)
  • Paragraph 2: First reason + evidence from passages or personal experience
  • Paragraph 3: Second reason + evidence
  • Paragraph 4: Acknowledge the other side and explain why your position is stronger
  • Paragraph 5: Conclusion — restate your position, don’t introduce new ideas
Use evidence from BOTH passages
  • Don’t just use the passage that agrees with you — engage with both
  • Quote or paraphrase specific details from each passage
  • You can agree with parts of the opposing passage while still arguing your position
  • Personal experience and knowledge can supplement passage evidence
Essay scoring — what scorers look for
  • A clear, specific central claim (not vague: “Both sides have good points”)
  • Evidence from BOTH passages cited specifically
  • Logical organization with transitions between paragraphs
  • Varied sentence structure and precise word choice
  • Minimal grammar, punctuation, and spelling errors
